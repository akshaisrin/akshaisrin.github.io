Background Information

In the mid-1900s, the Gold Rush caused immigrants from all over the world to flock
to the United States. Among these immigrants were people from Asia. “Asian”
Americans, as they were called, were not always the most liked group of people.
They were beat up, discriminated against, and even oppressed, for absolutely no
reason at all. Throughout history, there have been many examples of racism
and antibias towards these people. When they came to places such as California to
mine gold, they were attacked and were forced out of the mines.

There have been court cases, such as the 1854 case, People vs. Hal, where the
California Supreme Court established that Chinese Americans had no right to testify
against white citizens. There have been acts and laws, such as the 1882 Chinese
Exclusion Act, that prohibited the immigration of all people of Chinese ethnicity
from entering the United States. But what had to be the worst moment
in this long chain of horrifying events in American history, where racism and bias
clouded everyone’s judgement, happened around half a century later,
just after the bombing of Pearl Harbor in World War II.

The Moment

On December 7th, 1941, Japan bombed Pearl Harbor, a
United States naval base located in Honolulu, Hawaii. Japan
meant to destroy the U.S’s entire Pacific Fleet so that they
could gain an edge in the war. The U.S was shocked by the experience,
and they grow suspicious of people who they suspected could be
spies. Some people believed that people of Japanese descent living
in the U.S were enemy aliens and were spying on the U.S for Japan.

Just 2 months later, on February 19th, 1942, President Franklin D.
Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066 into law. This order gave
military commanders the power to exclude people from specific
areas. Though this law never specified any particular group of
people, it mostly affected Japanese Americans. Over the next few
years, over 110,000 Japanese Americans were sent to concentration camps.
Every 1 in 3 Japanese Americans died in these concentration camps,
through starvation, work, and even punishments. It would take a total
of 4 years before this act would be rescinded. 4 years full of
suffering, punishment, and captivity, just because people were of a
certain race.

The Impact
 Executive Order 9066 played a crucial role in the increase
of racial awareness in the U.S. On December 17th, 1943,
the Chinese Exclusion Act was lifted. The repealing stated that “...
the coming of Chinese laborers to the United States…[was]…hereby,
suspended…" (Repeal of the Chinese Exclusion Act, 1943). This was
just one year after Franklin D. Roosevelt signed Executive
Order 9066 in 1942. Chinese immigrants could now come
to the United States legally, which by itself, shows
an increase in racial awareness. This meant that
people were already starting to realize and
become more aware of the severe racial injustice
that they had been inflicting on “Asian Americans”
for the past century.

Another example of the increase
in racial awareness after Executive Order 9066 was
the Civil Liberties Act of 1988. This act stated that
“…a grave injustice was done to citizens and permanent
resident aliens of Japanese ancestry by the evacuation,
relocation, and internment of civilians during World War II…”
and that “…these actions…were motivated by racial prejudice,
wartime hysteria, and a failure of political leadership…”
(Civil Liberties Act, 1988). This act was the government’s
way of apologizing for incarcerating Japanese Americans
and acknowledging that what they did was wrong. To me,
this act shows that the government is now more aware
about the racism that has been occurring in American
all these years, and that they are going to take
immersive actions to ensure that history will
never repeat itself.
